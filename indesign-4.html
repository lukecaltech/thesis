Tour

Your tour begins at the former head of the Cannonball Trail. Allegedly used by General George Washington to move his cannonballs from the foundries of Ringwood to Morristown and West Point, the trail now spans the entire length of the Ramapo Mountains and runs through many different sections of the State Forest.

The trail was cleared in the twenties by the Boy Scouts of America and the New York-New Jersey Trail Conference. The trail was re-blazed in 2004 to accommodate the development of the Sullebarger housing complex (more of that later), which is where you’ll start.

The trailhead is located at the dead end of Barbara Drive. There will be a gate and plenty of “Private Property” and “No Trespassing” signs, but ignore them. The trail is public and starts to the left of the gate.

Walk past the gate and onto the flat paved section of the trail. The asphalt was poured by the Borough during well cleanup about ten years ago. The surrounding field is be ing cleared of the multiflora rose bushes which was cleared in an attempt to beautify the area, which ironically makes the remediation more visible.

Notice the rusting baseball backstop to your left in the middle of the multiflora remnants.

This is the former DuPont Field, which was leased to the town for use as athletic fields. The complex was closed in 1988 to the public after discovery of volatile organic compounds in the groundwater. The fences were added more recently to keep you out.

The field is now covered with a patchwork of native grasses and other low-lying foliage, broken up only by the occasional concrete pad and mysterious white PVC pipes sticking up out of the ground. These are groundwater

monitoring wells, which will become a familiar sight on DuPont property, which features hundreds of similar white PVC wells. Some are for monitoring, others inject chemicals to try to break down the toxins, and others still operate to pump-and-treat the contaminated groundwater directly. They are well guarded.

Walk across the field toward the hills. Once you enter the forest, you’re off the asphalt and officially climbing the Cannonball Trail. The trail follows the foundations of the old DuPont water system, which is why portions of it are sunken into the ground.
